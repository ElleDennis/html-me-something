Leaving on a Jet Plane

Alone, I walked through Philadelphia's airport. The corridors were long and not heavily populated at 10 p.m. on a Tuesday in January. My parents had driven me to the airport in St. Louis nearly 6 hours earlier and while no tears were shed as I waved good-bye, I now felt a little sad to leave home and them. But my journey had begun and I was enroute.

Each time I passed one of the huge glass airport windows, I lingered to catch the twinkle of outside lights, which clothed in velvety darkness, shined magically, richly. I silently questioned what and whom the lights illuminated. I wondered whether Philadelphia looked like St Louis or New York. Were the streets crowded with fast-moving and busy people? Or were the malls filled with people paused in front of store windows or seated inside restaurants eating quiche or burgers? Would I ever have the opportunity to escape the airport and visit Philadelphia? Or would this town and its airport only serve me as a stepping stone on my path to somewhere else?

I knew no one and spoke to no one until I found the gate from which the TWA flight to Paris would take off. After checking in and confirming my seat and the boarding time, I eyed an available spot on the carpeted floor and sat down with my legs stretched out and my back propped against the wall. I had a couple of Glamour Magazines and a paperback Jackie Collins romance novel. Between flipping a magazine's page, however, I occassionally glanced at the passengers seated at the gate. There weren't many young people. I wasn't surprised. It was mid-year and many college study abroad programs required a full year's residence. I had only decided to go abroad in the middle of the previous semester; my father and I were not getting along at all and since I was still living at home, I sought desperately to distance myself and find peace as soon as possible. Destined for southwestern France (could my choice have been more romantic?), L'université de Paul Valèry offered both study options to paying foreign students. I signed up and raced to fulfill all my home university's requirements for departure.

Suddenly, the voice on the loudspeaker beckoned passengers to board. I quickly gathered my purse, coat and reading materials to join the line. As I advanced, my heartbeat increased and I thought of arriving in France, a country I had often heard about, always dreamed of but had never seen. With the next dawn, I would breathe in French for the first time.
